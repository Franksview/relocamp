The Universal Playbook: How to Explore Any European City

It doesn't matter whether you land in a capital city or a port town you've never heard of. These strategies work everywhere, and they cost almost nothing.

1. Join a Free Walking Tour

This is the single best way to get your bearings in an unfamiliar city. Platforms like GuruWalk and Civitatis list tip-based walking tours in hundreds of European cities. You'll learn the history, get restaurant recommendations from a local, and meet other travelers — all within the first two hours of arriving. Show up, walk, listen, and tip what you think it was worth.

2. Find the Local Food Market

Every European city has one, and it's always worth finding. Whether it's an indoor market hall or an open-air Saturday market, this is where you taste the real city. Grab something you don't recognize, point at whatever the locals are eating, and have lunch for a fraction of restaurant prices. Markets are also perfect for picking up supplies if you're continuing your trip.

3. Rent a Bike for Half a Day

Most European cities now have affordable bike-sharing systems or rental shops near the center. A half-day on two wheels covers more ground than walking and gives you a completely different perspective. Cycle along the river, ride through the park district, find the neighborhood where tourists don't go. Two to four hours on a bike is the sweet spot — enough to feel like you've seen the city without exhausting yourself.

4. Embrace the Free Stuff

The best experiences in a new city rarely require a ticket. Here's your free-activity checklist:

  • Parks and gardens — Find the big central park and just sit. Watch the city happen around you.
  • Viewpoints — Climb the hill, find the bridge, take the elevator to the top floor. Every city has a spot with a view.
  • Neighborhoods — Pick a district that sounds interesting and walk it block by block. Street art, local shops, corner cafes.
  • Waterfronts — Rivers, harbors, coastlines. Walk along the water and let the city unfold beside you.
  • Churches and cathedrals — Usually free to enter, always architecturally stunning.

Drop-Off City Highlights: Five Cities Worth Discovering

These are some of the most common relocation drop-off points across Europe. Here's why each one deserves more than a quick handover and a dash to the airport.

Warsaw

A city of contrasts — rebuilt old town charm meets edgy Praga district street art. Incredibly affordable, with pierogi stands on every corner and the stunning Lazienki Park for a peaceful afternoon stroll.

Oslo

Skip the expensive restaurants and head to Aker Brygge waterfront or hike up to Ekeberg for panoramic fjord views. The Vigeland sculpture park is free, surreal, and unlike anything you've seen before.

Hamburg

Wander the Speicherstadt warehouse district, catch the Sunday Fischmarkt, and explore the gritty-creative energy of St. Pauli and the Schanzenviertel. The Elbe riverfront at sunset is unforgettable.

Naples

Chaotic, loud, beautiful. Eat the best pizza of your life for three euros, walk the Spaccanapoli, and take in views of Vesuvius from the waterfront. Naples rewards those who dive in headfirst.

Faro

The gateway to the Algarve that most people skip entirely. Explore the walled old town, walk the Ria Formosa boardwalk, and find an empty beach just a short bus ride away. Relaxed, sunny, and unpretentious.

Getting Home: Your Options After Drop-Off

So you've explored the city, eaten your weight in local food, and now it's time to head home. The good news: getting back from almost anywhere in Europe is easier and cheaper than you'd think.

  • Budget flights — Check Ryanair, Wizz Air, easyJet, and Transavia. Book a few days ahead and you can often fly home for under 50 euros. Google Flights is your best friend for comparing options.
  • FlixBus — The budget traveler's backbone. Connects hundreds of European cities with fares that start shockingly low. Overnight routes save you a night's accommodation too.
  • Trains — Comfortable, scenic, and often competitively priced if you book early. Check Trainline or national rail sites for deals.
  • Another relocation deal — Why not? If there's a campervan that needs to go in your direction, you could turn your return trip into the next chapter. Check Relocamp for deals departing from your current city.

Pro tip: Search for your return transport before you even start the relocation drive. Knowing your options in advance takes the stress out of the drop-off day and lets you focus on enjoying the city.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much time should I plan in my drop-off city after a campervan relocation?

We recommend at least one full day, ideally two. Most campervan drop-offs happen in the morning, giving you a full afternoon and evening to explore. If you book a budget hostel or Airbnb for one night, you can enjoy an unhurried next day before heading home.

Is it safe to leave luggage somewhere while I explore the drop-off city?

Yes. Most European cities have luggage storage options at train stations, and services like Bounce and LuggageHero offer lockers at shops and hotels across the city for a few euros per day. Drop your bags, travel light, and explore freely.

What if my drop-off city feels too expensive?

Even in pricier cities like Oslo or Copenhagen, free activities are everywhere: parks, waterfronts, architecture, street art, and free walking tours (tip-based). Local supermarkets and food markets are great for budget meals. The best city experiences rarely require a ticket.

Can I extend my campervan relocation to stay longer?

Relocation deals have fixed drop-off dates, so extending the campervan itself usually isn't possible. However, you can absolutely plan your own stay in the city after drop-off. Return the van, check into accommodation, and make the city your own for as long as you like.

How do I get home from the drop-off city?

You have plenty of options: budget airlines (Ryanair, Wizz Air, easyJet), FlixBus for affordable cross-Europe routes, trains via Trainline or national rail sites, or — our favorite — another campervan relocation deal heading back in your direction.
